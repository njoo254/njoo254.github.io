Technology isn't fun anymore



Hey guys. A couple weeks ago I was at an Airbnb trying to figure out what the $200 cleaning fee is supposed to cover if they're just gonna make me do a bunch of chores anyway, when we realized that episode four of "Severance" had just come out and what great timing this place had one of those beautiful 85-inch frame TVs that make it look like an art piece on the wall.

They had surrounds sound, they had a telescope pointed at the TV. I couldn't imagine a more perfect place to watch what would turn out to be a slightly confusing episode of television. So we turn it on and instantly we're like, "Why does this look like shit?"

I know that this TV costs thousands of dollars, and yet the moment anything moves on screen, it turns into this jumbled, blurry mess. But of course, because this kind of thing has been happening for years, we knew it was because of TrueMotion or MotionPlus or whatever this TV calls that feature where it smooths out every frame and makes everything you watch look like it was shot on an iPhone.

The best thing about this feature is that it's always on by default, and nobody I've ever met likes it. It's also named something different by every manufacturer, which makes searching the menus for it a little adventure every time. Now, I know I'm not even close to being the first person to complain about this feature.

It seems like everybody hates it, but that's also why it's so confusing to me. If so many people hate this, why, after over a decade, our TV manufacturers still forcing this down everyone's throats? How do they benefit from making their product look worse? I remember Ecstatically buying this Samsung in 2019 and almost returning it because the default settings made it look so bad I thought it was defective.

I just kind of sat there in denial about it for almost a week before being like, "This can't be right.

There has to be a setting I can change." Tragically though, a lot of people don't know about this setting. They don't realize their TV is actively sabotaging their viewing experience, and if this doesn't describe you, it probably describes someone you know. And by that, I of course mean your parents.

So sit down with them, explain what's going on, tell them why you get a headache whenever you watch TV with them and reiterate that it's not their fault.

Show them this video by a guy named Noodle. Let's say it's on twos. The AI doesn't smooth anything between the first and second frames of a single drawing because there's nothing to smooth.

They're the same picture. This means that what was originally a perfectly fine 12 fps animation is now a janky mess.

Or if they'd prefer to hear it from someone they know have Tom Cruise, explain it. - Video interpolation or motion smoothing is a digital effect on most high definition television. - I'm Tom Cruise. - Just to make sure they never find out about the whole Scientology thing. You cannot ruin top gun for your dad.

That movie's very important to him. Now, I will admit I was a little surprised reading some of the comments on this video. Seeing so many people disagree, I didn't know people liked it when the $100-million movie they're watching looks like a soap opera.

Now I understand of course, from a gaming perspective, people preferring higher frame rates, but I don't think that logic applies to movies. Games are interactive, so generally the smoother something is, the more responsive it feels.

But you're not interacting with a movie. You're just watching it. It doesn't need to be perfectly smooth. In fact, I think part of what makes a movie look like a movie is the sort of ethereal nature it has.

It doesn't look exactly like the way the world around you does through your own eyes, and I don't think it should.

I saw someone say they like it because it shows the flaws in movies you couldn't see at 24 fps, and it's like, no, the interpolation is creating those flaws.

It's not reverting a film back to its original settings. It's not a director's cut your TV's giving you private access to. It's just generating new frames based on what it thinks should go in between the existing ones. That's where the errors come from.

You're not watching a more authentic version of a movie because Sony MotionFlow added a bunch of blurry auras around every fast-moving object.

The most authentic version of a movie is watching it the way it was filmed. You shouldn't have to change anything, and I think more than anything else, that's what really aggravates me about this feature is the hubris of it.

There are so many deliberate choices that go into making a movie look a certain way. There's so many people involved in those decisions that know way more about cinematography than I do, so I trust their vision.

Why can't TV manufacturers do the same? Why do they look at a beautifully shot television show like it has a problem that they need to fix? Anyway, this experience got me thinking about new technology in a more general sense and how my excitement towards it has waned over time.

Sometimes, it seems like we kinda already figured everything out. You can communicate with anyone in the world instantaneously.

You have immediate access to every piece of music ever made. You can pay some guy to deliver a cold burrito straight to your door, and it only costs $70. This is the peak of humanity, baby.

There's nowhere else to go from here except backwards. So many things now seem to just invent a new problem so they can sell you the solution.

Or if we do create something novel and revolutionary, it's only gonna stay that way long enough for us to kill all our competition, allowing us to ruin our thing and make it infinitely more expensive.

There was a time where streaming services seemed like the final frontier of at-home entertainment, a marriage of cost and convenience that made all previous options obsolete, and my DVDs are just taken up space, and even if I wanted to watch something, I gotta get up, dust off my Xbox, navigate a bunch of menus, switch disks every four episodes. Netflix is so much better than this. Until of course they quadrupled their price and got rid of every show you ever wanted to watch.

So slowly but surely, a lot of people are moving away from the unreliable streaming services and going back to physical media.

Things like Blu-rays, vinyls, Tooth Tunes, and I've actually begun that migration process myself, but because I'm incredibly stupid, here's how it's going for me so far. I started watching "Friday Night Lights" for the first time in December.

It was something I had wanted to watch for years, but always put off, which is crazy because it's a show about football, which I like. With a bunch of music from Explosions in the Sky, a band I grew up listening to all the time. " It wasn't until a couple of months ago that I finally gave it a chance and got really into it 12 days before it was gonna be removed from Netflix. For the last two weeks of December, I binged as much of the show as I could as quickly as I could until around 3:00 AM on New Year's Eve.

13 minutes left in the season two finale, I was given an abrupt error message telling me that my beloved show was now gone. What, were they setting it on fire?

Fast forward a few more months to 2023 and Bally is filing for bankruptcy.

Well, it was nice while it lasted. Nope, they're still going. Somehow. Turns out I don't know what bankruptcy actually means.

What I do know is that this probably doesn't bode well for the growing list of technical problems. They apparently don't have the money to address.

I should make a video about that. I know that this is one very specific gripe about a very specific problem, but I feel like there's so many things like this now.

So many companies that seem to get their rocks off from making us have to jump through hoops in order to do anything. They're constantly merging with some other company so they can both be sold to a third company. Content bounces around from streaming service to streaming service like a hot potato. We tried to streamline everything and only made it more complicated. I can't keep up.

Now, back to the article. I know that part of the feeling I'm about to describe is just because of my diminished sense of wonder as I've aged, but I feel like there used to be a time when technology was a lot more exciting to me. I mean, what could be more fun than a gadget?

Well, besides a gizmo, of course. But it seemed like most things that came out had a genuine purpose, that there was more of a collective desire to push humanity forward, to provide solutions to actual problems.

And I know that this is all still happening, but it seems like so many innovations now are just new ways to manipulate people or spy on people or replace people all together.

I'm not excited about what might be created in the future as much as I am fearful of it, I'm fearful of what AI is threatening to do to every job on earth. I'm fearful of all these humanoid robots that keep popping up.

What if one of them tries to kill me? Or worse, what if we fall in love? How am I supposed to explain that to people? For every OLED Steam Deck and surprisingly powerful tiny Mac computer, there seemed to be 10 wearable AI companions or a TV that's free because it stops showing you ads.

Or what if you could wear your phone on your head so it could never stop showing you ads?

I remember when touchscreen smartphones came out and I practically started drooling at the idea.

This device, which I hate more than anything in the world, is incredible. It's truly amazing what this thing is capable of. But now the question is, where do you go from here? The jumps in phone technology have become pretty unnoticeable despite how hard they're trying to get us to notice them.

When we went from landlines to cell phones, that was crazy. When they added a full keyboard so you didn't have to spend 30 seconds typing one word. That was crazy. And then they added email and a really good camera and a touch screen and a GPS and the internet and a compass. The improvements between video game consoles used to be astronomical. Now I end up on a side by side comparison video squinting as hard as I can, trying to spot any difference.

In 1995, video games look like this. 10 years later, they look like this. 10 years after that, they look like this. And in the 10 years' sense, they look like or wait, which one is the new one? And I know I'm viewing this from a consumer standpoint.

I'm completely oblivious to the strides being made in medicine and science and interplanetary defense mechanisms. But it just seems like whenever a new thing comes out, we have to pay a lot for very little. Technology has gone so far in the wrong direction that people are now trying to find ways to go back.

Physical media is making a comeback. Dumb phones are making a comeback.

As people become more disillusioned to the future that was promised to us, the best way to break out of it is to recapture a sense of simplicity. And not all of this is just nostalgia. I think some of it speaks to the fundamental difference in how things are made now. You used to be able to own things. Now almost everything is borrowed.

You're not buying a product, you're subscribing to a service. I don't own a bunch of music because I'm paying Spotify every month. I'm just temporarily given access to music until I stop paying and then have nothing to show for it.

And even if you do buy a physical product, there's no guarantee it's gonna stay in the condition it was in when you bought it. A lot of things now can be remotely updated or even deactivated.

The Spotify car thing completely bricked when they stopped wanting to deal with it. 

A few months ago, Roku forced an update onto all of their TVs making it so you can't turn the motion smoothing feature off. This company just announced they're gonna start charging a monthly fee so anyone who already bought their thermostat can continue to use it.

We are buying things that can be made worse or taken away from us whenever the manufacturer feels like it. So what are we even buying anymore?

These things aren't designed to last because the entire economic system in this country is based on perceived shareholder value.

If I sell you a product that you only have to buy once and then get to keep it forever, how will I get more money from you in the future? How will line keep go up if transaction over? So things break or become obsolete?

A lot of tech devices are more affordable than they used to be, but they're also more disposable.

The interface on your four-year-old smart TV suddenly takes 10 times longer to navigate. Your iPhone 12 suddenly crashes five times a day. No, we're not doing that on purpose, but it does kinda seem like a sign that it's time to buy a new one, right? And this isn't a conspiracy or an exaggeration. Technology is making us dumber.

And the more we're conditioned to accept this relationship, the easier it is to sell us a AI agent that does all of our thinking for us and that we learn to trust implicitly.
